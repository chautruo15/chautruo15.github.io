Research

Throughout my time as an undergrad, I have engaged in various research projects within the Department of Physics at Princeton University.

As a summer intern at the Princeton Gravity Initiative from June to July 2023, I worked under Dr. Delilah Gates and studied X-ray reflection spectroscopy, a technique used to measure the spin of a black hole (BH) that involves simulating and observing the Fe-Kα line emission from the BH’s accretion disk. My project focused on modeling these broadened emission spectra using the open-source code Adaptive Analytical Ray Tracing (AART). AART simulates high-resolution images of BH accretion disks, calculating the disk radius from which observed photons were emitted and the redshift factor of the photons at each pixel on the image. Using these values obtained from AART, I implemented code to calculate the resulting emission spectrum for a given set of disk parameters, such as the BH spin and the inclination angle of the observer. To decrease the runtime when computing the spectra, I also helped to alter AART to create a polar grid rather than a Cartesian grid when simulating images. This research was extended into my Junior Fall Independent Work, where I modeled spectra for the accretion disk model of sub-Keplerian flow, in which the angular momentum from the standard model of circular Keplerian flow is rescaled.

For my Junior Spring Independent Work, I worked under Professor Adam Burrows from the Department of Astrophysical Sciences to study the long-term stability and evolution of compact multi-planet systems. Using REBOUND, an open-source N-body code, I simulated the evolution of various planetary systems, modeling the time to instability as a function of the initial orbital separation between planets. Additionally, I examined the semi-major axes and orbital eccentricities of the planets to identify ejections and uncover trends that might predict when and how these ejections occur.

During the summer of 2024, under the guidance of Dr. Alejandro Cárdenas-Avendaño at the Princeton Gravity Initiative, I developed code in Julia to calculate the inspiral orbit and gravitational waveforms of extreme mass ratio inspiral (EMRI) systems using the numerical kludge method. Beginning with code to integrate the geodesic equations, I expanded it to incorporate radiation force calculations, allowing me to model the inspiral. I then extended the code to compute the gravitational waveform and produce its “audio,” ultimately creating animations of the inspiral orbits with the sonified waveforms. For my Senior Thesis, I am collaborating with Professor James Stone at the Institute for Advanced Study and Hengrui Zhu at the Princeton Gravity Initiative to implement algorithmic improvements to the moving-puncture approach for binary black hole (BBH) simulations within the AthenaK framework, a modern implementation of Athena++. The project focuses on enhancing the slow-start lapse technique to improve the accuracy and stability of BBH simulations.

Animations

The following animations showcase the inspiral orbits and gravitational waveforms of EMRI systems that I produced during my summer research at the Princeton Gravity Initiative from June to July 2024. In developing these, I implemented the numerical kludge method to simulate the inspiral and calculate the waveform, which I then sonified to reflect the frequency evolution over time. These animations combine a visual representation of the orbit and the corresponding sound, offering an engaging perspective on the dynamics of EMRI systems.

This animation illustrates an example of a geodesic path, representing the trajectory of a test particle orbiting a 106 M☉ supermassive black hole (SMBH) without any external forces.

This animation shows the inspiral orbit of an EMRI system with a mass ratio of 1:100 and a SMBH spin of 30%, after taking into account the effects of radiation reaction.

This animation depicts the inspiral orbit and waveform of an EMRI system with a mass ratio of 1:100 and a SMBH spin of 30% with orbital parameters eccentricity e = 0.0, semilatus p = 20.0, and inclination angle ι = π / 2, accompanied by sound that reflects the waveform’s frequency evolution.

This animation depicts the inspiral orbit and waveform of an EMRI system with a mass ratio of 1:100 and a SMBH spin of 30% with orbital parameters e = 0.7, p = 5.0, and ι = π / 2, accompanied by sound that reflects the waveform’s frequency evolution. Due to the more eccentric orbit of this system, the waveform shows sharp pulses at each pericenter passage, producing a distinct “spiking” effect in the audio.
